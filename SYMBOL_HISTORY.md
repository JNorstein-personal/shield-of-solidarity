Symbol History

Origin

The Shield of Solidarity (Against Hate & Narrowmindedness) was created by Joshua Norstein in June 2026.

The symbol emerged from a period of reflection on privilege, visibility, and allyship. The creator recognized that, as a masculine-presenting white man, he could often move through public spaces without attracting the forms of suspicion, discrimination, hostility, or political targeting experienced by many people he loved and identified with.

The Shield of Solidarity was conceived as a visible rejection of that neutrality and as a public declaration of support for communities facing such treatment.

Its purpose was not to speak on behalf of those communities, but to communicate a conscious and deliberate willingness to stand beside them.

Influences

The design combines visual elements drawn from several established traditions of community symbolism.

Primary influences include:

The Intersex-Inclusive Progress Pride Flag designed by Valentino Vecchietti, which itself builds upon Daniel Quasar's Progress Pride Flag and Morgan Carpenter's Intersex Flag.

The black and brown chevrons incorporated into the Progress Pride tradition to recognize and affirm Black, Indigenous, and other People of Color whose experiences are inseparable from broader struggles for equality and inclusion.

The rainbow infinity symbol commonly associated with neurodiversity advocacy and autistic self-advocacy.

The Shield of Solidarity was created as a transformative synthesis of these symbolic traditions.

Its purpose is not to replace any of the symbols from which it draws inspiration, but to serve as a complementary emblem of allyship and solidarity among the communities those symbols represent and those who choose to stand with them.

It is intended as a contribution to an evolving tradition of openly shared community symbolism rather than an attempt to claim ownership over or authority over any of the communities reflected in its design.

Public Domain Dedication

From its inception, the symbol was intended to remain freely available for public use.

Accordingly, upon publication in June 2026, the creator dedicated the symbol to the public domain under CC0 1.0 Universal and publicly disclaimed any intent to claim exclusive trademark rights in the symbol.

The dedication was made to encourage broad adoption, adaptation, and participation while discouraging future attempts to restrict or monopolize the symbol's use.
